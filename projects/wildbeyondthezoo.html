Rosamond Gifford Zoo: Wild Beyond The Zoo

Collaborative design-thinking project developed for the Rosamond Gifford Zoo in Syracuse. Reimagining the zoo's brand as a citywide experience, using storytelling and interactive design to extend conservation messaging beyond zoo gates.

Role
Designer · Researcher
Type
Academic · Real Client
Duration
15 Weeks
Tools
Figma · InDesign · Research
Project Overview
A 15-week real-client design challenge for the Rosamond Gifford Zoo, structured around three major phases. Groups were reshuffled with each phase — we repeatedly inherited another team's work, built on their research, and pushed the project forward. This mirrored real professional handoffs and taught us to collaborate across teams, absorb new information quickly, and maintain continuity.
My Contributions
Research lead · secondary research · stakeholder interview · pamphlet interior layout · brand guidelines · strategic planning
Outcome
Two-part campaign · citywide scavenger hunt · social media advertising · cohesive conservation brand experience
01 — Context & Process

A semester-long, real-client design challenge

Each week, our group developed inherited ideas, pushed them forward, and presented findings showing clear progress. The structure mirrored real professional handoffs — teaching us how to collaborate across teams, absorb new information quickly, and maintain continuity in a long-term project.

Design Thinking Framework

"Instead of moving through these steps in a straight line, we constantly cycled back to earlier discoveries — revisiting research, reframing problems, and integrating new insights as they emerged."

02 — Our Approach

Problem finding, not just problem solving

This semester pushed us beyond traditional problem-solving and into problem finding — a deeper, more investigative approach to design. Instead of simply responding to what the client asked for, we learned to look beneath their initial requests to uncover root issues.

Ask better questions
We learned to question surface-level briefs and dig into what the client truly needed versus what they initially asked for.
Interview real people
Primary research with real visitors and stakeholders gave us nuanced insight no secondary source could provide.
Visit firsthand
We repeatedly visited the zoo to experience it ourselves — grounding our design decisions in lived reality, not assumptions.
03 — Teamwork & Collaboration

One class, one team

Teamwork this semester went beyond our assigned groups — our entire class functioned as one collaborative team. We constantly checked in with classmates who previously handled our project and ensured we could present the client with five unique solutions, each targeting a different issue.

Within our small groups, we organised meeting times, divided tasks, and brainstormed together each week. Clear communication, flexibility, and mutual support mirrored how real design teams operate across shifting roles and shared responsibilities.

04 — Research

Understanding why people don't visit the zoo

To better understand visitor behaviour, I took the lead on research — conducting targeted secondary research and a follow-up interview with someone deeply familiar with the zoo experience: a person who visited throughout childhood, worked with the County Zoo, and now works at a nonprofit supporting the zoo's mission.

This allowed us to uncover nuanced insights into both visitor behaviour and the challenges faced by zoo staff, complementing our team's broader research findings.

05 — Iteration & Pivot

Inheriting a concept — and knowing when to change direction

In the final phase, our new team inherited a concept from a previous group. Through research and stakeholder constraints, we found ourselves needing to make a pivot in both concept and language.

Inherited concept
Social media polls and challenges to encourage community participation and self-identification with the zoo's brand.
The problem
The concept leaned too heavily on social media and was lacking visual coherence and community rootedness.
The pivot
Shifted the zoo's storytelling beyond digital platforms and into the Syracuse community itself.
The refinement
With one week remaining, we identified visual inconsistency issues and fully refined the concept for final presentation.
06 — Final Direction

Wild Beyond The Zoo — a citywide campaign

The final solution became a two-part campaign: a simplified scavenger hunt placed in familiar Syracuse locations, paired with a supporting social media advertising campaign. Together, these elements create a cohesive brand experience that uses storytelling to highlight the zoo's commitment to conservation, education, and community connection.

07 — Design Decisions

Focused choices, clear rationale

01
Campaign Strategy

Scavenger hunt over digital-only

Problem  A purely digital campaign wouldn't reach people who aren't already engaged with the zoo online.

Decision  Physical stickers and pamphlets placed at familiar Syracuse locations bring the zoo into everyday life.

Why it works  Meeting people where they already are creates organic brand discovery without requiring existing awareness.

02
Storytelling

Conservation through narrative, not lecture

Problem  Conservation messaging often feels distant or preachy — failing to create emotional connection.

Decision  The campaign leads with animal stories and local connections rather than statistics.

Why it works  Narrative-first messaging builds empathy and makes conservation feel personal and relevant.

03
Visual Design

Brand consistency across physical and digital

Problem  Previous iterations had visual inconsistency between physical materials and social content.

Decision  Developed unified brand guidelines covering colour, type, and illustration style used across all touchpoints.

Why it works  Cohesive visual language builds recognition and trust — essential for a public-facing brand campaign.

04
Community Rooting

Syracuse-specific placement strategy

Problem  Generic campaign placements fail to feel local or personally relevant to Syracuse residents.

Decision  Sticker and advertising locations were strategically selected based on high foot-traffic Syracuse landmarks.

Why it works  Local specificity signals that the zoo is part of the Syracuse community — not just an attraction to visit once.

Final Deck
Final Deliverables

I led the design of the pamphlet's interior layout and conducted supporting research to validate our solution. I also contributed to strategic discussions around sticker placement, advertising locations, and the development of the campaign's brand guidelines.

08 — Reflection

What this project taught me

This project reinforced the importance of strong research as the foundation for design-driven solutions — learning how to ask better questions, identify real problems, and uncover meaningful insights. It taught me how to adapt within shifting team dynamics, stay flexible through constant changes, and collaborate effectively under pressure.

"Perseverance and clear communication can transform uncertainty into a strong, thoughtful outcome."

Personal Takeaways
  • Problem finding is as important as problem solving — dig beneath the brief
  • Real research with real people surfaces insights no secondary source can provide
  • Adapting to inherited work and shifting teams mirrors real professional practice
  • Visual cohesion and strategic clarity are inseparable in brand-level design work
