Project Overview
Craft Connect began as a collaborative team project in a software design course, scoped to centralize learning, material exchange, and community-building for hobbyist crafters. After the course ended, I revisited the concept independently — refining scope, redesigning for mobile, and translating the idea into a polished, accessible product design case study.
Team Phase
Concept definition · requirements gathering · system architecture · frontend implementation with React + JavaScript
Solo Phase
UX redesign · interaction design · mobile-first · Figma high-fidelity · accessibility implementation
01 — Problem Statement

A fragmented, frustrating experience for hobbyist crafters

Many people interested in creative hobbies face scattered resources, unclear entry points, and communities that feel unwelcoming. There was no single place to learn, share, and connect.

Beginners
Struggle to find clear starting points and trustworthy tutorials — overwhelmed before they even start.
Experienced Crafters
Accumulate leftover materials with no easy, local way to share or exchange them.
Craft Communities
Scattered across forums and social platforms, with poor moderation and low discoverability.

"How might we design an app that centralizes learning, material exchange, and community-building for crafters of all levels?"

02 — User Needs

What real users needed most

From research and discussion, four consistent needs shaped every design decision that followed.

🌱
Low-pressure discovery
New crafters need to explore projects without feeling overwhelmed or judged by engagement metrics.
📦
Local material exchange
Users with extra supplies want a simple, location-aware way to share or trade with nearby crafters.
🤝
Safer community space
Community members want positive, moderated interactions — not the toxicity of mainstream social platforms.
♿
Universal accessibility
Clear navigation and accessible design regardless of technical experience or ability level.
03 — Technical Foundation

Building to learn, not just to polish

As part of the team phase, we built an early version of Craft Connect to validate technical feasibility. The goal was insight over polish — understanding how proposed features behaved under real constraints.

05 — Design Decisions

Focused choices, clear rationale

01
Core App Flows

Narrowing to three essential flows

Problem  The original scope tried to do too much at once, creating cognitive overload for new users on first use.

Decision  The redesign centres on exactly three flows — discovering projects, exchanging materials locally, and finding tutorials. Every screen maps to one of these.

Why it works  Focused flows reduce decision fatigue and make the app immediately learnable, especially for beginners who need a clear path forward.

02
Interaction Design

Removing the "like" button — intentionally

Problem  Traditional engagement mechanics create competitive dynamics that discourage beginners from sharing their work.

Decision  The home screen features saves and ratings — but no likes or counts that gamify participation.

Why it works  Removing vanity metrics directly protects the low-pressure environment users needed. A values-driven decision, not just a simplification.

03
Information Architecture

Progressive disclosure over feature overload

Problem  First-time users encountered too many options simultaneously, leading to analysis paralysis.

Decision  Wireframes and screens were designed with progressive disclosure — complexity revealed only when users are ready for it.

Why it works  Beginners land on simple, clear screens. Power features are accessible but never imposing.

04
Accessibility

Accessibility in the UI — not just in the docs

Problem  The original app documented accessibility requirements but didn't consistently implement them in the actual design.

Decision  Final Figma designs enforce high colour contrast, clear typographic hierarchy, simplified iconography, and comfortable spacing on every screen.

Why it works  Accessibility has to be expressed visually to be real — making the app genuinely usable for all skill and ability levels.

Wireframes
Final UI

The home screen displays algorithm-recommended projects, showing the user both the project's rating and the estimated time needed to complete it.

Users can also save projects to revisit later. The "like" feature was intentionally left out to avoid creating the kind of toxic, competitive environment often found on social media.

The Guide section features a variety of beginner-friendly instruction guides in text format. When a user highlights a hyperlinked keyword or important term, a pop-up page appears with a brief summary of the topic.

This page may also include user-submitted tips or input, along with a helpful video to provide visual support and make the instructions easier to follow.

06 — Reflection

What this project taught me

Revisiting Craft Connect allowed me to practice stepping back from a broad concept and making focused, justified product decisions. The gap between the original implementation and the redesign was instructive — not because the first version failed, but because building it revealed exactly what needed to change.

"Start with systems thinking, then refine toward clarity, usability, and feasibility."

If I continued this project
  • Conduct usability testing specifically with beginner crafters to validate the low-pressure onboarding experience
  • Further refine onboarding flows based on real observational feedback — not assumptions
  • Explore moderation tooling to support healthy, positive communities as the platform scales
  • Test the "no likes" decision with users — validate whether it genuinely reduces anxiety or creates new confusion
